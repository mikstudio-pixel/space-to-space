The Kiko Hari project, developed by Valérie Taftová for the PV268 Digital Design course, is a conceptual toy designed to alleviate a specific and common childhood anxiety: the fear of exposed feet at night. The project is based on the "universal rule" that keeping one's feet covered by a blanket provides a magical barrier that prevents monsters from reaching the sleeper.

Concept and Functionality

The toy, named Kiko Hari, is a specialized doll designed to physically and psychologically secure a child’s sleeping environment. Its primary functions are:

Securing the Blanket: The doll is designed to hold or "secure" the blanket in place throughout the night, ensuring the child's feet remain covered and "safe" from imaginary monsters.

Providing Light: Kiko Hari emits a gentle, soothing light, acting as a nightlight to further diminish the fear of total darkness.

Comforting Aesthetic: The doll wears an outfit explicitly inspired by pajamas, reinforcing its role as a nighttime companion and encouraging a sense of shared routine with the child.

Visual Identity and Design

The project’s visual presentation emphasizes a soft, approachable, and whimsical aesthetic. The character of Kiko Hari features a round, friendly face and a soft-textured body, making it suitable for a child to keep in bed. The sketches and photographic documentation show the toy in a domestic setting, highlighting its integration into a child's bedroom.
Educational and Emotional Impact

Like the other projects in this series, Kiko Hari uses "magical thinking" to solve a practical emotional problem. By turning a simple object—a blanket—into a reinforced "shield" through the presence of the doll, Taftová’s design empowers children to manage their own nighttime anxieties. The project succeeds in taking a relatable, small-scale fear and addressing it through a blend of physical product design and empathetic storytelling.
