The Digital Design (PV268) course at the Faculty of Informatics at Masaryk University. It is a comprehensive studio-based program that challenges students to integrate physical fabrication with interactive digital content. The core objective of the course is to move beyond traditional graphic design into the realm of user-centered design and prototyping, requiring students to create complex artifacts that bridge the gap between the physical and virtual worlds.
The Integrated Design Process

Students followed a plan that combined research, physical creation, and digital extension:

Physical Prototype and Packaging: A major component of the course involved the creation of a physical toy or artifact designed to address specific psychological needs or "fears". Students were required to design not only the object itself but also its professional packaging, considering how branding and physical materials influence user experience.

Fabrication and Materiality: The curriculum emphasizes "learning by doing" through various fabrication techniques. Students utilized 3D printing, laser cutting, and traditional hand-crafting (such as sewing or crochet) to build their prototypes, as seen in projects like Esmeralda Pérez and Connie.

A critical part of the later weeks involved brainstorming and prototyping how a character and its packaging could exist in an immersive environment. Students created VR storyboards and interaction flows, focusing on narrative clarity and emotional impact.

Narrative and Psychological Purpose

The overarching theme of the student work was the use of design as a tool for empathy and problem-solving. Each student identified a specific childhood fear—ranging from dental procedures and structural instability to the dark and household chores—and developed a "guardian" character to mitigate that anxiety.

Connecting the Classroom to "SPACE TO SPACE"

The connection between these student works and SPACE TO SPACE lies in the definition of design as a response to profound systemic and personal challenges. The exhibition call defines space not just as a physical volume, but as a context for life that encompasses both micro and macro scales, as well as physical and mental dimensions. The students' projects directly inhabit this "mental dimension" by creating artifacts that transform a child’s psychological space—turning a dark bedroom or a scary construction site into a managed environment of safety and heroism.

Furthermore, the exhibition advocates for design that is "humane, empathetic, and socially conscious," which mirrors the course's objective of using user-centered design to solve emotional problems. By equipping a "mail robot" like Martin with night-vision goggles to navigate scary household chores , or designing Kiko Hari to physically secure a blanket against "monsters", students demonstrated the "shared responsibility" and "new visions of life" requested by the exhibition. Both the course and the exhibition view the designer as a navigator who uses innovative technology—whether VR, 3D printing, or immersive storytelling—to help people inhabit a constantly evolving and often intimidating world.
